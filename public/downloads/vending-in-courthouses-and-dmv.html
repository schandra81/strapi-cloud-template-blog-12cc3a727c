Combined Courthouse + DMV Vending Pack

State-procurement scope, captive-audience planogram, multi-language signage template

Courthouses and state DMV offices share a common operating profile — long captive dwells, state-procurement procurement paths, and a multilingual public. They also share the same constraint: people waiting hours with no easy exit. This pack covers the combined state-procurement framework, a captive-audience planogram, and multi-language signage that meets state language-access requirements.

1. The captive-audience profile

DMV wait times in major states average 30-90 minutes; renewal / appointment-on-day-of waits can run 2-4 hours. Courthouse juror waits run 4-8 hours per session; public docket-wait runs 1-3 hours. Both audiences cannot easily leave and return — meal-replacement-weighted planograms and reliable hydration outperform pure snack-mix configurations.

2. State-procurement scope — paths to vending placement

Both courthouses (state + county) and DMV offices typically procure under one of three paths.

State master vending contract
Most states maintain a master vending contract on the central procurement schedule. State agency facilities (DMV, state-funded courthouses) can ride the master. Streamlines path; pricing is pre-negotiated.
Cooperative procurement (state COG / OMNIA / Sourcewell)
Many states + counties access cooperative purchasing schedules. Sourcewell, OMNIA Partners, NASPO ValuePoint commonly include vending services schedules.
Local RFP / concession agreement
County courthouses + smaller DMV / motor-vehicle offices often issue their own concession RFP. Operator pays commission (8-25% typical) to the agency / facility.
Commission revenue handling
State commission revenue typically flows to a general fund or a building-maintenance account. Document the destination at contract sign — avoids audit issues at year-end.

4. Multi-language signage — state language-access requirements

Many states require public-facing services (including DMV) to provide language access for top non-English languages spoken in the jurisdiction. Vending signage should match this requirement.

California (DMV reference)
Top languages: English, Spanish, Chinese (Traditional + Simplified), Korean, Vietnamese, Tagalog. Signage in English + Spanish minimum; additional languages where local demographics support.
Texas
English + Spanish minimum at DMV / courthouse public-facing placements.
New York
Executive Order 26 + Local Law 30 (NYC) — top 10 languages for city; state agencies follow comparable practice. English + Spanish + Chinese + others per locality.
Florida
English + Spanish minimum at DMV / courthouse public-facing placements; Haitian Creole in South Florida.
Federal courthouse / federal building
LEP Executive Order 13166 — meaningful access for limited-English-proficient persons. English + most-common local non-English language at minimum.

5. Multi-language signage template — content elements

  • How to operate (insert card / tap / select / dispense).
  • How to request refund (QR code + phone number + clerk-of-court / customer-service link).
  • Allergen summary (FALCPA — 9 major allergens listed on machine face).
  • Nutrition info access (QR code to per-SKU nutrition page).
  • ADA accessibility statement + operator contact for accommodation requests.
  • Cashless payment options accepted (card brands, mobile wallet).
  • Pricing display in machine face (high-contrast, ≥ 5/8 in characters per ADA Section 703).

6. Security + access protocols (shared)

Both venues have contractor-access protocols and packaging restrictions for security perimeters.

  • Operator route drivers background-checked + badged through facility contractor program.
  • Restock cart / dolly screened at entry (X-ray + visual).
  • Restock schedule coordinated with peak windows (avoid morning DMV opening rush, court-call mornings).
  • Cashless-primary or cashless-only reduces cash-handling security overhead.
  • E-lock with audit log; tamper-resistant fasteners.
  • Glass-free packaging at courthouse placements (see courthouse pack); DMV typically allows glass but plastic preferred.
  • Cellular telemetry — not facility Wi-Fi.

7. ADA + accessibility (Title II + state regulations)

  • Reach range 15-48 in AFF (ADA Section 308).
  • 30 in × 48 in clear floor space (Section 305); doesn't block accessible queue or path-of-travel.
  • Operating force ≤ 5 lbf (Section 309.4).
  • Cashless option for users without coin/bill handling capability.
  • High-contrast pricing, ≥ 5/8 in characters.
  • Tactile or braille pricing where state code requires.
  • Operator's ADA attestation + annual recertification on file.

8. Reporting + governance

  • Monthly revenue + commission report to facility / agency.
  • Quarterly uptime + SLA report.
  • Refund rate + reason codes.
  • Stockout incidents on high-velocity days.
  • Language-access compliance attestation (signage in current state-required languages).
  • ADA + cleaning attestation current.
  • Operator's contractor-badge roster current.
