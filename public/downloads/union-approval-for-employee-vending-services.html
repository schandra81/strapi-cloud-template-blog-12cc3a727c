Union-Coordinated Vending Services Pack

CBA provision review, RFP criteria, labor-management committee governance

Vending program decisions in a unionized facility are governed by the collective bargaining agreement (CBA) and the labor-management committee. Bringing in vending without coordination triggers grievances under NLRA Section 8(a)(5) (refusal to bargain over mandatory subjects). This pack frames the CBA provisions to review, the joint-decision RFP scoring rubric, and a governance structure that survives the next contract cycle.

1. NLRA framing — why coordination is non-optional

Under NLRA Section 8(a)(5), employers must bargain in good faith over mandatory subjects of bargaining — including conditions that affect terms and conditions of employment. Vending services have been treated as a permissive subject in many cases, but specific elements (meal-break access, on-site food availability, pricing of subsidized SKUs, cashless-payroll integration) routinely cross into mandatory territory. The safe operating posture is joint coordination with the labor-management committee.

2. CBA provisions to review before issuing an RFP

Pull the current CBA and locate each of these provisions. Note section number for the RFP appendix.

Meal and break period
Defines unpaid meal break (typically 30 min) and paid rest breaks (typically 15 min × 2). Vending placement + access during these windows is the central operational question.
Food service / cafeteria provisions
Many CBAs reference 'reasonable on-site food availability.' Vending often satisfies this requirement; the contract should make this explicit.
Wage deduction / payroll integration
Any cashless system that integrates with payroll (badge-pay) is a mandatory subject. Requires CBA amendment or side-letter agreement.
Health and safety
Hot-food vending, allergen labeling, refrigeration requirements may map to existing H&S provisions.
Subcontracting / outsourcing
Some CBAs restrict bringing in contractors. Vending operators usually fall outside this language, but verify.
Labor-management committee scope
The standing committee that reviews shop-floor issues. Make vending a standing agenda item.

Establish a vending sub-committee or add vending as a recurring item on the existing labor-management committee. The structure below is standard.

5. Contract clauses to include in the operator agreement

  • Labor-management committee receives quarterly performance report (uptime, stockouts, refunds, planogram velocity).
  • Pricing on a defined subsidy basket (water, fresh fruit, baseline meal) requires committee notice before change.
  • Planogram refresh proposals reviewed with committee before execution; committee has consultative input (not unilateral approval) on SKU mix.
  • Worker grievances about vending escalate via committee, not solely via operator's customer service.
  • Operator route drivers / restock crew labor practices disclosed annually.
  • Cashless / badge-pay system, if implemented, requires a side-letter agreement signed by both parties.
  • Operator commits not to use the breakroom space for advertising contrary to bargained terms (e.g., political content, anti-union messaging).

6. Implementation timeline — typical 90-day joint process

  1. Days 0-30 — committee formation + CBA review

    Stand up vending sub-committee. Review CBA provisions. Document shared baseline (current pain points, worker survey).

  2. Days 30-60 — joint RFP issuance

    Co-developed RFP issued to 3-5 operators. Joint walk-through of facility. References + pricing reviewed jointly.

  3. Days 60-75 — operator interviews + reference checks

    Operator candidates present to full committee. Reference checks include union counterparts at reference sites where possible.

  4. Days 75-90 — joint selection + side letter

    Joint recommendation memo to facility manager + union local. Side letter (if cashless / payroll integration) drafted and ratified.

7. Worker communication — joint messaging

Communications about the program should be co-signed or jointly cleared. Sole-management messaging about a jointly-decided program is a common source of friction.

  • Launch announcement co-signed by HR + union local.
  • Refund / grievance procedure posted on machine face with both management + union contact info.
  • Quarterly worker satisfaction snapshot — 3-question intercept survey, results shared with committee.
  • Pricing changes communicated ≥ 30 days in advance with rationale.
