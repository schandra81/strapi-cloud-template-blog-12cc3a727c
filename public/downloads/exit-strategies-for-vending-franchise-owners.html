Vending Franchise Exit Playbook

Preparation timeline, valuation methodology, broker engagement

Selling a vending franchise — single-unit or multi-unit — works best when the operator starts the preparation cycle 18-36 months before listing. This playbook codifies the preparation timeline, the four valuation methodologies buyers apply, broker engagement structure, and the FDD + franchise-transfer-approval mechanics that govern most franchise exits.

2. Financial preparation

Buyers value vending businesses on adjusted EBITDA over 2-3 trailing years. Clean financials + reasonable add-backs add significant multiple uplift. Aggressive or unsupportable add-backs do the opposite.

  1. Three years of clean P&L

    GAAP-leaning P&L, separated by location + machine + product category. Cash + cashless mix split. Commission paid to host visible per location.

  2. Normalized add-backs

    Owner compensation above market, personal vehicle / phone / travel, one-time legal + advisor fees, family-payroll above market. Each add-back documented with supporting paperwork.

  3. Owner-dependent revenue reduction

    Owner-only relationships migrated to staff or operations manager 12+ months before exit. Buyer discounts revenue tied solely to seller relationship.

  4. Quality of earnings (QoE) — buyer side

    Buyer typically commissions QoE from accounting firm during due diligence; seller-side QoE 6-12 months before list smooths the buyer's review.

  5. Tax structure

    Asset sale vs equity sale carries materially different tax outcomes; consult tax counsel 18+ months before listing.

4. Value drivers — what moves the multiple

  • Long-term host contracts with assignability — multi-year contracts surviving sale add 0.5-1.0x to the multiple
  • Telemetry + KPI transparency — operator dashboards demonstrating uptime + sell-through reduce buyer uncertainty premium
  • Diversified host portfolio — no single host > 15% of revenue reduces concentration discount
  • Younger equipment fleet — avg age < 7 years; ENERGY STAR-aligned reduces near-term capex needed
  • Trained operations team independent of owner — reduces owner-dependence discount
  • Clean financials + minimal add-backs requiring explanation — reduces buyer / lender friction
  • Growing trend line (revenue + EBITDA) over trailing 24-36 months — multiple uplift
  • Established franchisor relationship + good standing — reduces transfer-approval risk

5. Broker engagement

  1. Broker vs M&A advisor

    Single-unit or sub-$1M revenue: business broker (BizBuySell + IBBA-affiliated). Multi-unit or $1M+: lower-middle-market M&A advisor (boutique firm with vending + route-based experience).

  2. Engagement letter terms

    Success fee 8-12% (broker) or 4-7% (M&A advisor) on transaction value. Minimum fee + retainer typical. Tail period 12-24 months on introduced buyers.

  3. CIM (confidential information memorandum)

    8-15 page CIM covering business overview, financials, host portfolio, equipment fleet, growth runway. NDA-gated; teaser distributed broadly first.

  4. Buyer outreach

    Strategic buyers (national + regional vending operators), private equity (vending + route-based platforms), individual buyers (SBA-financed). Broker manages parallel processes for competitive tension.

  5. LOI review + selection

    Multiple non-binding letters of intent (LOIs) evaluated on price, structure (asset vs equity), holdback / earn-out terms, transition support, financing certainty. Best price ≠ best LOI.

6. Franchisor transfer approval

Most vending franchises (Healthier4U, NaturalsToYou, Reis & Irvy's, Naturals2Go) include franchise-transfer-approval clauses in the FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document) Item 17. Approval is required for sale; seller cannot close without it.

  • FDD Item 17 — transfer conditions, transfer fee (typically $5K-$25K), buyer qualifications, training requirements
  • Right of first refusal (ROFR) — many franchisors have ROFR; seller must offer the deal to the franchisor at the LOI terms before closing with the third-party buyer
  • Buyer financial qualification — franchisor reviews buyer net worth + credit; typical thresholds match initial franchisee requirements
  • Buyer training — buyer + key staff complete franchisor training program before close or within 90 days after
  • Transfer fee + lease consents — transfer fee paid to franchisor; equipment leases + financing assigned with consent of lender + lessor
  • Seller release + non-compete — seller signs general release of franchisor + agrees to franchise-area non-compete (typically 2-5 yr radius)
  • Timing — franchisor approval cycle typically 30-90 days; build into closing schedule
