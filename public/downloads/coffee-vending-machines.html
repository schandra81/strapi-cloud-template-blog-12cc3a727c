Coffee Vending Machine Selection Playbook

Category-by-category sizing, water filtration, maintenance cadence, and OCS-vs-DIY framework

Choosing a coffee machine is the most over-engineered and under-measured workplace decision: getting it right means happy employees and a clean QBR; getting it wrong means a broken bean-to-cup unit, a $4/cup capsule program, and Slack complaints. This playbook codifies category sizing, water filtration, maintenance cadence, and the OCS-vs-DIY framework — referenced against NSF/ANSI 42 + 53 + 372 (water), NEC 210.8 GFCI, and the SCA (Specialty Coffee Association) brewing standard (10:1 to 18:1 brew ratio, 195-205°F).

2. Sizing — cups per machine per day

Rule of thumb: 1.5-2.5 cups per employee per workday in the U.S. workplace (NCA generation-cohort data). Size for the 80th-percentile peak, not the average.

Pod / capsule throughput cap
~120 cups/day before queueing complaints; descale every 200-400 cycles.
Single-cup bean-to-cup throughput
~600 cups/day with twin grinders + auto-clean; daily milk-system rinse if milk-equipped.
Commercial bean-to-cup throughput
Up to 250 cups/hour peak; SCA-spec brew temperature 195-205°F and brew ratio 1:15 to 1:18.
Brew-by-pot throughput
1.5 gallons per 4-minute cycle = ~24 cups; works best for breakroom-side coffee station with airpots.

3. Water filtration (the #1 reliability lever)

  1. Why filtration matters

    Hardness > 100 ppm CaCO3 scales boilers and clogs solenoids; chlorine taints flavor. Most premature bean-to-cup failures trace to skipped filtration.

  2. Standards reference

    Inline filter certified to NSF/ANSI 42 (taste/odor + chlorine reduction), NSF/ANSI 53 (heavy metals + cysts), and NSF/ANSI 372 (lead-free contact). Replace per manufacturer schedule, typically every 6 months or 5,000-10,000 gallons.

  3. Hardness target

    Target 50-100 ppm CaCO3 for espresso/bean-to-cup; below 30 ppm risks corrosion and flat extraction. Add a remineralization stage if RO is used.

  4. Plumbing requirements

    Dedicated 3/8 in cold-water line with shutoff; backflow preventer per local plumbing code. Drain proximity for pulled-shot bean-to-cup units.

  5. Electrical requirements

    20A 120V dedicated circuit per single-cup bean-to-cup; 30A 208V for many commercial units. GFCI per NEC 210.8 in wet locations.

5. OCS vs. DIY framework

Office Coffee Service (OCS)
Operator owns equipment, breakdown service, water filter changes, descaling, supply replenishment, and refund flow. Predictable monthly per-cup or per-machine fee; typical SLA 24-48 hr response. Equipment refresh every 5-7 years.
DIY (admin orders pods)
Admin orders supplies; in-house facilities or external tech handles breakdowns. Lower fixed cost; higher hidden cost in admin time + machine downtime + lost-day productivity.
Hybrid
Employer owns the equipment; operator provides break/fix + filter + descale + supplies under a service contract. Common in headquarters environments where equipment is part of the brand-system.
Break-even line
OCS is typically more economical above ~75 employees once admin time, downtime, and consumable-pricing leverage are accounted for.

6. Quality + experience controls

  • SCA brew-ratio target 1:15 to 1:18 weight-based for filter coffee; 195-205°F brew temperature
  • Bean freshness — within 4-8 weeks of roast date; rotate bean SKUs quarterly so the program doesn't stale
  • Milk handling — refrigerated ≤41°F per FDA Food Code 2022 §3-501.16; daily flush of milk lines
  • Decaf, non-dairy (oat, almond, soy), and unsweet/half-sweet syrup coverage
  • Allergen signage on flavored / dairy options (FALCPA + FASTER Act top-9)
  • Refund/credit flow for failed brews — kiosk / app / SMS / on-machine button
  • Cup, sleeve, stir-stick, sugar/sweetener, napkin par levels audited weekly
