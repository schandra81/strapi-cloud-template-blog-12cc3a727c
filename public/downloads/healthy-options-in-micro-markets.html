Micro-Market Healthy-Options Playbook

Assortment, planograms, dietitian coordination, labeling, dietary coverage, and measurement

This playbook codifies the operating practices that turn 'healthy options in the market' from marketing copy into a measurable program. It maps to the FDA FALCPA + FASTER Act allergen rules, 21 CFR 101.65 nutrient-content claims, the 2020-2025 Dietary Guidelines for Americans, the FDA menu-labeling rule for chains of 20+ (21 CFR 101.11), and the NEMS-V healthy-vending scoring methodology used in academic and public-health programs.

1. Healthy-share definition

Use a single, defensible 'healthy-share' definition across procurement, planogram, signage, and reporting. Mixing definitions is the most common reason healthy programs fail audit.

2. Planogram and placement

  1. Eye-level rule (Thorndike et al., Harvard 2014/2020)

    Place 35-50% of eye-level facings on the healthy-share definition. Eye-level lifts healthy-share velocity 10-30% without subsidy changes.

  2. Endcaps + reach-zone

    Reserve the right-hand reach zone (most-shopped side) for fresh-food and healthy-share. Audit quarterly with planogram photography.

  3. Coffee + water adjacency

    Place water + unsweetened beverages adjacent to the coffee station and fresh-food cooler — the highest-traffic dwell points.

  4. Choice architecture signage

    Green-leaf icon + 'Better-for-you' shelf strip on every qualifying facing. NEMS-V scoring rewards consistent visual cueing.

3. Dietitian coordination model

Registered dietitian (RD/RDN)
Engage a Commission on Dietetic Registration-credentialed RDN for planogram review, allergen-claim attestation, and quarterly assortment audit. Typical engagement: 4-8 hours/quarter.
Audit deliverables
Healthy-share % per category, dietary-coverage gap analysis, signage-claim review, recommended adds/drops with rationale.
Wellness-program tie-in
RDN report is the artifact wellness teams use to justify the micro-market as a wellness benefit and as data for biometric / outcomes reporting.
Operator-side dietitian (alternative)
Many operator partners retain an in-house RDN whose attestation is delivered with each quarterly business review.

4. Labeling and claim compliance

  • FALCPA + FASTER Act top-9 allergen statement on every SKU (milk, egg, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, sesame)
  • FDA Nutrition Facts panel (21 CFR 101.9) on every packaged SKU; updated label format with added sugars line
  • Calorie disclosure on standard-menu items if the operator falls under 21 CFR 101.11 chain coverage (20+ locations)
  • Health and nutrient-content claims limited to those defined in 21 CFR 101.13 + 101.65 ('low sodium,' 'healthy,' etc.)
  • Shelf signage matches package label — no claims on the shelf strip that aren't on the package
  • Allergen filter active in the kiosk software (365 Retail Markets, Avanti, Three Square) so members can sort by allergen

6. Measurement and reporting

  1. Healthy-share velocity (monthly)

    Healthy-share units sold ÷ total units sold. Target ≥30% within 6 months, ≥40% within 12 months. Pulled from kiosk POS telemetry.

  2. Healthy-share facings (quarterly)

    Healthy-share facings ÷ total facings, audited via planogram photo. Target ≥35%.

  3. NEMS-V score (annual)

    Run the NEMS-V (Nutrition Environment Measures Survey — Vending) audit annually. Targets vary by program; ≥75% is a defensible 'healthy environment' threshold.

  4. Member NPS for assortment

    Quarterly 1-question pulse: 'The market gives me healthy options I want to eat.' Top-2-box target ≥75%.

  5. Dietitian attestation

    Quarterly RDN report attached to the QBR deck.
