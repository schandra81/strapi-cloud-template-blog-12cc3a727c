Campus Specialty Vending Placement Guide

Categories, costs, compliance, and vendor selection

Use this guide to scope specialty vending placements that go beyond snack + beverage — harm-reduction, hygiene, OTC medicine, study supplies, PPE, allergen-aware, exam-supplies, and laundry. Each category has distinct compliance overlays, equipment requirements, and vendor-selection criteria. Capability framing throughout — describe what operators are equipped to provide rather than claim work at named campuses.

3. Compliance overlay checklist

  • FDA + state-board approval where category warrants (OTC, harm-reduction, refrigerated cold-chain).
  • Expiration-date tracking + restock-time first-in-first-out verification for any consumable with expiry.
  • Tamper-evident packaging per FDA 21 CFR 211.132 for any health-category SKU.
  • Allergen labeling per FALCPA (21 USC §343(w)) on any food SKU; allergen-aware planogram for explicit allergen-aware machines.
  • ADA Section 308 reach range maintained for all selection buttons + payment readers.
  • Section 305 clear floor space (30" x 48") at every machine.
  • FERPA-aligned data handling on any campus-card payment integration.
  • Local health department permit where TCS food sold from refrigerated unit.

4. Vendor + operator selection criteria

  1. Specialty SKU sourcing

    Operator demonstrates vendor relationships for the specific categories in scope. Generic vendors lack relationships with harm-reduction, OTC, or allergen-aware distributors.

  2. Compliance track record

    Operator has documented FDA / health-department compliance history; sample audit-response runbook.

  3. Telemetry + expiration tracking

    Operator's telemetry platform supports SKU-level expiration tracking (where consumables have expiry). Generic snack-vending telemetry often lacks this.

  4. Restock cadence

    Specialty categories often have higher peak demand windows (PPE pre-class, exam supplies mid-finals, Narcan after public-health events). Operator commits to surge-restock protocol.

  5. Pricing transparency

    Specialty SKUs are scrutinized by student government + dean of students. Operator's pricing model published; cap on margin during exam periods for exam supplies.

  6. Subsidy model support

    Operator supports zero-cost or subsidized-cost transactions where category warrants (harm-reduction, hygiene, food-security).

5. Implementation roadmap

Stage 1 — needs assessment (Month 1)
Survey student government, dean of students, student health, residence life; identify top 3 specialty categories by need.
Stage 2 — compliance scope (Month 2)
Engage campus FDA / health-service contact, sustainability office, accessibility office; document compliance requirements per category.
Stage 3 — operator selection (Month 3)
Issue specialty-vending RFP; score per Section 4 criteria.
Stage 4 — pilot (Months 4-6)
1-2 unit pilot per category in highest-need location; gather student + staff feedback; refine planogram.
Stage 5 — scale (Months 6-12)
Roll out to full footprint; integrate with institutional reporting (food-security, harm-reduction outcomes).
Stage 6 — annual review (Year 2+)
QBR with operator; refresh category mix; sunset under-utilized categories; expand demonstrated-need categories.

6. Reporting + measurement

Utilization
Per-machine velocity by SKU; surge-window capture; uptime + stockout incidents.
Outcomes (institutional aggregate)
Cross-tab with retention, persistence, wellness-services utilization where appropriate. Always aggregate; never individual-level.
Equity
Access by college / residence hall / time-of-day; identify under-served populations.
Compliance audit
Annual expiration + tamper-evident audit; documented in operator's audit-response runbook.
