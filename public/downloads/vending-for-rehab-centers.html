Rehab Center Vending Implementation Pack

Clinical coordination workflow, planogram by population, SLA framework

Rehab centers — inpatient rehab facilities (IRF), skilled nursing (SNF), substance-use disorder (SUD) facilities, behavioral health, and pediatric / adolescent rehab — operate under tight clinical control over food and beverage. A vending placement here is not a standard break-room install. This pack covers the clinical coordination workflow, population-specific planogram, and SLA framework tuned for rehab.

1. Rehab populations + clinical constraints

Each rehab population has distinct dietary, behavioral, and safety constraints. The planogram, packaging, and machine choice must reflect them.

2. Clinical coordination workflow

Every rehab vending placement needs sign-off from clinical leadership. Build the workflow into the contract.

  1. Clinical sponsor

    Medical director (IRF / SNF) or program director (SUD / behavioral) sponsors the placement and signs off on planogram framework.

  2. Registered Dietitian review

    RD reviews planogram against population's dietary plan; flags SKUs to exclude (high sodium, dysphagia risk, food-allergy clusters). RD review repeated quarterly.

  3. Behavioral health / SUD review

    Where applicable, behavioral health clinical lead reviews for ligature, sharps, food-substitute concerns. Adolescent unit may require additional restrictions.

  4. Patient safety committee

    Placement goes to patient safety committee for one-time approval + annual re-review.

  5. Family + patient handout

    If patients are independent shoppers, provide a family handout describing the framework, the safe SKUs, and the dietary considerations.

  6. Incident pathway

    Define the path for any vending-related clinical concern (e.g., dysphagia near-miss, allergen exposure) — typically incident reporting per Joint Commission PI.01.01.01.

4. Equipment selection — behavioral + ligature considerations

Standard vending equipment carries multiple ligature anchor points (handles, hooks, cord-routes). Behavioral health placements need a hardened review.

Cabinet mounting
Floor-anchored (≥ 4 anchor points) per OSHA 1910.176 + NAMA tip-prevention; no exposed handles or hooks on cabinet exterior.
No glass bottles
Exclude glass-bottle SKUs from behavioral + adolescent placements; specify aluminum cans and rigid plastic only.
Power cord
Routed inside wall via NEC-compliant raceway; not exposed in patient area. Coordinate with facilities per IBC + NFPA 70.
Touchscreen vs button
Some behavioral programs prefer non-touchscreen to reduce risk; verify with clinical lead.
Dispense bin design
Anti-ligature dispense flap; no protruding spring or hook.
Service access controls
Service door requires staff escort during operator visits in behavioral / SUD / adolescent settings.

5. Payment + cost handling

Rehab patient access to cash varies; many SUD and behavioral facilities restrict personal cash. Payment design needs to fit.

Cashless + family-account
Family / guarantor preloads patient account; patient redeems via fob or PIN. Avoids cash-on-person issues.
Hospital-issued credit
Per-patient allowance (e.g., $20/week) loaded onto fob; covers staple SKUs.
Free baseline + paid premium
Water + crackers free (facility funded); premium SKUs paid via family account.
Staff-only badge
Staff badge unlocks staff-priced SKUs in staff break-room machines.
Caregiver / visitor cashless
Standard cashless (Apple Pay / Google Pay / contactless card) on visitor-facing units.

7. Allergen + labeling discipline

Rehab populations frequently include patients with documented food allergies. FALCPA + state code applies + rehab clinical protocols may add restrictions.

  • FALCPA 9 major allergens (21 USC §343) labeled on every SKU: milk, egg, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, sesame (added 2023).
  • FDA Food Code §3-602.11 vending allergen-summary sign on machine face.
  • Per-SKU allergen sticker visible through glass (operator standard practice).
  • Clinical RD reviews allergen mix at quarterly QBR; flags any concentration concern (e.g., high peanut concentration in pediatric unit).
  • Allergen-incident protocol: any patient allergen exposure logged per Joint Commission PI.01.01.01 + reviewed by clinical team.
  • Operator product recall protocol: 24-hour pull of any recalled SKU per FDA / USDA recall notification.
